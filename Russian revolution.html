📖 Socialism in Europe and the Russian Revolution
└─ 📝 The Age of Social Change
• Impact of the French Revolution on Social Change
The French Revolution (1789–1799) marked a turning point in European history by challenging the traditional structure of society, which was divided into estates (clergy, nobility, and commoners). It introduced the ideas of freedom, equality, and individual rights. These ideas inspired debates about societal change in Europe, Asia, and colonies like India, where figures such as Raja Rammohan Roy and Derozio discussed their significance. The revolution also led to the emergence of political ideologies such as conservatism, liberalism, and radicalism, which represented varying responses to the need for social transformation.
└─ 📝 Liberals, Radicals and Conservatives
• Political Ideologies: Liberals, Radicals, and Conservatives
After the French Revolution, three distinct political ideologies emerged in Europe: Liberals, Radicals, and Conservatives. Liberals advocated for individual rights and parliamentary governance but preferred gradual reforms rather than revolutionary change. Radicals sought a complete restructuring of society, emphasizing equality and universal suffrage. Conservatives, on the other hand, resisted change and aimed to preserve traditional institutions like the monarchy and the church. These ideologies shaped debates on societal transformation during the 18th and 19th centuries, influencing movements in Europe, Asia, and colonies like India. Key figures such as Raja Rammohan Roy in India discussed the significance of these ideas, inspired by the French Revolution.

└─ 📝 Socialism in Russia
• The Russian Revolution and the Rise of Socialism
The Russian Revolution of 1917 was a pivotal event that led to the establishment of socialism in Russia. It occurred in two major phases: the February Revolution and the October Revolution. The February Revolution (March 1917 in the Gregorian calendar) led to the abdication of Tsar Nicholas II and the end of the Romanov dynasty, which had ruled for over 300 years. This resulted in the establishment of a Provisional Government. However, the October Revolution (November 1917 in the Gregorian calendar), led by the Bolshevik Party under Vladimir Lenin, overthrew the Provisional Government and established a socialist state. The Bolsheviks aimed to create a classless society based on the ideas of Karl Marx, emphasizing the redistribution of land, nationalization of industries, and workers' control over production. This revolution marked the beginning of the Soviet Union, which lasted until its dissolution in 1991.
└─ 📝 A Turbulent Time: The 1905 Revolution
• The 1905 Revolution in Russia: Causes and Key Events
The 1905 Revolution in Russia was a pivotal moment in the country's history, marked by widespread social unrest and demands for political change. Key causes included the defeat of Russia in the Russo-Japanese War (1904-1905), which weakened national morale, and the economic hardships faced by workers and peasants. The revolution began with the event known as 'Bloody Sunday' on January 22, 1905, when peaceful protesters led by Father Gapon were fired upon by the Tsar's troops in St. Petersburg, resulting in hundreds of deaths. This massacre sparked nationwide strikes, peasant uprisings, and demands for reforms. In response, Tsar Nicholas II issued the October Manifesto on October 30, 1905, promising the creation of a Duma (parliament) and granting limited civil liberties. Despite these concessions, the revolution ultimately failed to bring significant change, as the Tsar retained substantial power and repression continued.
└─ 📝 The First World War and the Russian Empire
• Impact of the First World War on the Russian Empire
The First World War (1914–1918) had a profound impact on the Russian Empire. Russia entered the war as part of the Allied Powers but faced severe economic, social, and political challenges. By 1917, the strain of the war led to widespread discontent among soldiers, workers, and peasants. Key events include the February Revolution of 1917, which resulted in the abdication of Tsar Nicholas II and the end of the Romanov dynasty. The Provisional Government, which replaced the monarchy, struggled to address war-related issues, leading to the Bolshevik Revolution in October 1917. The Bolsheviks, led by Vladimir Lenin, withdrew Russia from the war by signing the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk with Germany in March 1918, ceding significant territories. These events marked the beginning of the Soviet era and the end of imperial Russia.

📖 The Revolution of October 1917
└─ 📝 Main Topic
• The October Revolution of 1917
The October Revolution, also known as the Bolshevik Revolution, occurred in Russia on October 25, 1917 (Julian calendar; November 7, 1917, Gregorian calendar). It marked the overthrow of the Provisional Government by the Bolshevik Party, led by Vladimir Lenin. This revolution was a key event in the Russian Revolution, leading to the establishment of a socialist state. The Bolsheviks seized power in Petrograd (modern-day Saint Petersburg) with the support of workers, soldiers, and peasants. The revolution was driven by widespread dissatisfaction with World War I, economic hardship, and demands for land redistribution. Key institutions involved included the Petrograd Soviet and the Red Guards. This event laid the foundation for the creation of the Soviet Union in 1922.

└─ 📝 Stalinism and Collectivisation
• Stalinism and Collectivisation: Key Policies and Impact
Stalinism refers to the policies and practices of Joseph Stalin, the leader of the Soviet Union from 1924 to 1953. A major aspect of Stalinism was the policy of collectivisation, implemented in the late 1920s and early 1930s. Under collectivisation, private farms were abolished, and peasants were forced to work on collective farms (kolkhozes) controlled by the state. By 1929, Stalin announced the 'Dekulakization' campaign, targeting wealthier peasants (kulaks) who resisted collectivisation. Millions of peasants were displaced, and widespread resistance led to violent crackdowns. The policy resulted in a severe famine between 1932 and 1933, particularly in Ukraine, where it is estimated that 3-5 million people died. Despite the human cost, Stalin claimed collectivisation was necessary to modernize agriculture and support industrialisation. This policy remains a critical topic for understanding the social and economic transformations in the Soviet Union during Stalin's rule.

The USSR, formed in 1922, became a superpower and actively promoted socialism in Europe, Asia, and beyond during the 20th century. Key events include the Comintern's establishment in 1919 to support global communist movements and the USSR's role in World War II, which solidified its influence. The Cold War (1947–1991) further highlighted the USSR's global impact as it competed with the USA in spreading its ideology.

‚Ä¢ The Indian Desert: Key Features

It is characterized by arid conditions, sandy terrain, and extreme temperatures. The desert receives less than 150 mm of rainfall annually, making it one of the driest regions in India. Vegetation is sparse, consisting mainly of thorny bushes. The Luni River is the only significant river in this region. The Indian Desert forms a natural boundary between India and Pakistan.

The Western Coastal Plains include important features such as lagoons and backwaters, especially in Kerala. These plains are crucial for trade and transportation, with major ports like Mumbai, Chennai, and Kochi located along the coastline.

The width of the Shiwaliks varies between 10 to 50 km, and their average elevation ranges from 900 to 1,200 meters above sea level. Geologically, the Shiwaliks are composed of unconsolidated sediments brought down by rivers from the higher ranges, making them prone to erosion. They are the youngest part of the Himalayas and are characterized by narrow valleys called 'duns,' such as the Dehradun Valley.

‚Ä¢ Bhabar: The Northern Plains Zone
Bhabar is a narrow belt located at the foothills of the Himalayas in the Northern Plains of India. It is characterized by coarse sediments such as pebbles and boulders deposited by rivers descending from the mountains. This zone is typically 8 to 16 km wide and lies parallel to the Shiwalik range. Due to the porous nature of the soil, streams disappear in this region, making it unsuitable for agriculture. Bhabar is an important geographical feature to understand the transition between the Himalayan Mountains and the Northern Plains.

Bhangar refers to the older alluvial soil found in the Northern Plains of India. It is characterized by its higher concentration of kankar (calcareous deposits) and is less fertile compared to the newer alluvial soil, known as Khadar. Bhangar is typically found in elevated regions of the plains and is formed over long periods of time. This soil type is important for understanding the agricultural and geological features of the Northern Plains.

Khadar refers to the newer, fertile alluvial soil found in the floodplains of the Northern Plains of India. It is formed by the deposition of silt brought by rivers during annual flooding. This soil is highly suitable for agriculture, especially for crops like wheat and rice. The Northern Plains are geologically recent landforms, and Khadar plays a crucial role in supporting India's agricultural economy.

‚Ä¢ Formation and Characteristics of Sand Dunes
Sand dunes are landforms commonly found in desert regions, such as the Indian Desert (Thar Desert). They are formed by the deposition of sand carried by wind (aeolian processes). The size and shape of sand dunes vary based on wind speed, direction, and the availability of sand. In India, the Thar Desert contains extensive sand dunes, which are dynamic and constantly reshaped by wind patterns. These dunes play a vital role in understanding desert ecosystems and are important for studying soil erosion and wind movement.

‚Ä¢ Barchans: Crescent-shaped Sand Dunes
Barchans are crescent-shaped sand dunes commonly found in desert regions, such as the Indian Desert. These dunes are formed by wind action and typically have their horns pointing downwind. They are a key feature of arid landscapes and are indicative of wind direction and intensity. Barchans can vary in size, with some reaching heights of several meters and lengths of up to 30 meters. Understanding their formation helps in studying desert geomorphology and wind patterns.
‚îî‚îÄ üìù Longitudinal Dunes
‚Ä¢ Longitudinal Dunes in the Indian Desert
Longitudinal dunes are sand formations found in the Indian Desert, specifically in the Thar Desert region. These dunes are elongated ridges of sand that run parallel to the prevailing wind direction. They are formed due to consistent wind patterns over time, which shape the sand into linear structures. Longitudinal dunes are typically several kilometers long and can reach heights of up to 100 meters. Their formation is influenced by arid climatic conditions and sparse vegetation, which allows sand to accumulate and shift freely.

The Luni River is the largest river in the Indian state of Rajasthan that flows westward and ends in the marshy lands of the Rann of Kutch in Gujarat. It originates from the Pushkar Valley of the Aravalli Range near Ajmer. The river is approximately 495 kilometers long. Despite being a seasonal river, it plays a crucial role in the arid region of Rajasthan. The name 'Luni' is derived from the Sanskrit word 'Lavanavari,' meaning 'salt river,' due to its saline water content, especially in the lower reaches. Students should remember its geographical significance and its role in supporting agriculture and settlements in the desert region.

The Andaman and Nicobar Islands are a group of 572 islands located in the Bay of Bengal. They consist of 36 islands, with a total area of approximately 32 square kilometers. Coral islands are ecologically significant as they support diverse marine life and act as natural barriers against coastal erosion.
‚îî‚îÄ üìù Barren Island (Active Volcano)
‚Ä¢ Barren Island: India's Active Volcano
Barren Island, located in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, is the only active volcano in India and South Asia. It is situated approximately 135 km northeast of Port Blair. The volcano first erupted in 1787 and has had multiple eruptions since then, with the most recent one recorded in 2017. The island is uninhabited due to its harsh volcanic terrain. The volcanic activity is attributed to the tectonic movement in the Andaman Sea region, which lies on the boundary of the Indian and Burmese tectonic plates.

Q1: Explain the geological significance of the Himalayan Mountains and their impact on the Indian subcontinent. IMPORTANT 5 Marks
Answer:
The Himalayan Mountains are geologically significant as they are young fold mountains formed due to the collision of the Indian Plate with the Eurasian Plate around 50 million years ago. This tectonic activity makes the region geologically unstable, leading to frequent earthquakes and ongoing mountain-building processes. The Himalayas act as a natural barrier, influencing the climate of the Indian subcontinent by blocking cold winds from Central Asia and trapping the monsoon winds, which results in heavy rainfall in the northern plains. Additionally, the Himalayas are the source of major rivers like the Ganga, Brahmaputra, and Indus, which sustain agriculture and support millions of people. Their rugged terrain and high altitudes also make them a biodiversity hotspot and a critical region for ecological balance.
Q2: Describe the three parallel ranges of the Himalayas and their key features. IMPORTANT 4 Marks
Answer:
The Himalayas consist of three parallel ranges that run longitudinally:
1. **The Great or Inner Himalayas (Himadri):** This is the northernmost range and the highest, with an average height of 6,000 meters. It includes some of the world's tallest peaks, such as Mount Everest and Kanchenjunga. The range is perpetually snow-covered and is the source of many glaciers and rivers.
2. **The Lesser Himalayas (Himachal):** Located south of the Himadri, this range has an average height of 3,700 to 4,500 meters. It is characterized by forested hills, valleys, and hill stations like Shimla and Darjeeling. The region is known for its moderate climate and agricultural activities.
3. **The Shiwaliks (Outer Himalayas):** The southernmost and lowest range, with an average height of 900 to 1,200 meters. It consists of unconsolidated sediments and is prone to erosion. The Shiwaliks are known for their wide valleys, called 'Duns,' such as Dehradun and Patli Dun, which are fertile and densely populated.
